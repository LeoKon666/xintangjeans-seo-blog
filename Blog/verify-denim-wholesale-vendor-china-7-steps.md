## Step 1: Verify Physical Factory Existence (Not Just Registration)

Many "manufacturers" on Alibaba are trading companies renting office space near actual factories. Here's the truth: China's business registration system makes it incredibly easy to get a manufacturing license without owning a single sewing machine. We've audited suppliers whose "factory address" turned out to be a 12th-floor apartment with three employees and a laptop.

### ✔️ Documents to Request:
- **Business License** (Check registration address matches factory location)
- **Export Registration Certificate** (Verify through China Customs query)
- **Production Floor Photos** (Must show workers/equipment, not empty buildings)

### ⚠️ Red Flags:
- Supplier refuses video call or live factory tour
- Address on documents is a residential area or office park
- Unable to provide worker ID cards or payroll records

**Pro Tip:** Cross-check the factory address using satellite view on Baidu Maps (China's Google Maps). Look for industrial buildings with loading docks—small workshops can't handle bulk orders. The factories that stand out are usually massive complexes with dedicated shipping areas you can spot from aerial view. If the address shows a six-story commercial building in a business district, you're dealing with a middleman.

## Step 2: Conduct a 4-Point Sample Test

Samples tell you more about a supplier than any contract. Most buyers make the mistake of visually inspecting a sample and calling it good. That's not enough. Test them like this:

| Test Type | Method | Pass Criteria |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| **Wash Durability** | Home wash sample 5x | No color bleeding, seam twisting |
| **Stitch Quality** | Stretch waistband 100x | No popped stitches |
| **Fabric Weight** | Weigh on kitchen scale | Within 5% of promised GSM |
| **Metal Components** | Check zippers/rivets | YKK or equivalent brand marks |

For the wash test, use cold water and hang dry—this is harsher than most consumer washing and reveals construction flaws fast. If the inseam twists by more than 1 inch after five washes, the cutting was done improperly. For stitch quality, literally grab the waistband and pull it apart 100 times like an aggressive customer trying on jeans. Weak stitching will fail before you hit 50 pulls.

**What we've seen consistently:** Suppliers who cut corners on samples will cut 10x more corners in bulk production. Reject any sample failing 2+ tests. Yes, this means you might go through four or five suppliers before finding one that passes. That's the whole point.

## Step 3: Audit Their Quality Control Process

Most suppliers will claim they have "strict QC." Ask these specific questions during negotiation, and you'll separate professionals from amateurs within five minutes:

1. **"Walk me through your QC checklist for bulk orders"** (Good answer includes pre-shipment inspection reports)
2. **"How do you handle defective units?"** (Should offer 1:1 replacement, not just discount)
3. **"Show me your measurement tolerance standards"** (Legit factories document ±1cm rules)

Why does this matter? Because a supplier without documented QC standards doesn't actually have QC—they're just inspecting finished products visually and hoping for the best. A real factory will send you their actual inspection sheet showing 15-30 checkpoints with pass/fail criteria written down. 

**Case Example:** One Xintang jeans factory we audited had a 22-step QC process including laser measurement of 18 key points, metal detector scan for broken needles, and wash color matching under D65 light (the industry standard lighting). Another supplier we rejected claimed they "checked everything" but couldn't produce a single document proving it.

**Cost of skipping this:** 15-30% defect rates are common with unchecked suppliers. On a 1,000-piece order, that's 150-300 jeans you can't sell.

## Step 4: Validate Fabric & Trim Suppliers

Cheap denim often stems from unauthorized fabric substitutions. Verify these three things before signing anything:

1. **Denim Mill Certificates** (Ask for ISO-certified mills like Black Peony or Prosperity)
2. **Trim Purchase Records** (Real factories keep invoices for zippers/thread)
3. **Wash House Documentation** (Chemical compliance certs for stone/acid washes)

To be fair, most buyers never think to ask about fabric sourcing. They assume the factory handles everything. But here's what actually happens: you approve a sample made with premium Japanese denim from Kurabo Mills, and the bulk order arrives using mystery fabric from an uncertified mill in a rural province. The jeans look similar enough, but after three washes the color fades unevenly and the hand-feel is completely different.

**🚨 Critical Warning:** Many "factories" buy pre-made jeans from unknown workshops and simply relabel them. Always ask for raw fabric rolls photos before cutting. A legitimate factory will show you their fabric inventory room with rolls labeled by mill name and lot number.

## Step 5: Check Hidden Compliance Risks

These issues surface only after customs seizure, which means your inventory is stuck in a warehouse racking up storage fees while you scramble to prove compliance. Most buyers get this wrong because they think compliance only matters for big brands.

1. **Forced Labor Screening** (Xinjiang cotton bans affect export eligibility)
2. **Chemical Compliance** (REACH, CPSIA reports for lead/phthalates)
3. **Ethical Certifications** (BSCI, WRAP audits validate working conditions)

**Stat to Know:** 14% of denim shipments to EU/US faced compliance holds in 2025 according to SGS reports. That's 1 in 7 orders getting delayed or rejected, often because the supplier used dyes containing restricted chemicals or couldn't prove their cotton source.

For first-time buyers, this feels overwhelming. Start with two questions: "Do you have current REACH test reports?" and "Can you prove your cotton isn't from Xinjiang?" If the supplier hesitates or says they'll "get that later," walk away.

## Step 6: Payment Structure That Protects You

Never pay 100% upfront. That's the number one rule. Demand this timeline:

1. **30% deposit** after sample approval
2. **60% balance** after passing pre-shipment inspection 
3. **10% retention** held until delivery confirmation

The logic here is simple: if the supplier knows they're getting 100% payment before shipping, they have zero incentive to maintain quality during production. But if you're holding back 10% until you confirm the goods arrived as promised, they'll pay attention to details.

**Payment Method Safety Rankings:**
1. Letter of Credit (LC)
2. Escrow (Alibaba Trade Assurance)
3. PayPal Goods & Services
4. ⚠️ Never use Western Union or direct wire for first order

Yes, the MOQ matters, but payment terms matter more. We've seen buyers negotiate great prices only to lose everything because they wired $18,000 via Western Union to a "factory" that disappeared the next day.

## Step 7: Verify Shipping & Customs Readiness

Many quality suppliers fail at logistics, and your perfect jeans arrive damaged because nobody bothered to use corner protectors in the carton. Confirm these three capabilities before finalizing your order:

1. **EXW/FOB Experience** (Can they properly pack for ocean freight?)
2. **Export Documentation** (Commercial invoice, packing list templates)
3. **HS Code Knowledge** (Proper classification avoids duty surprises)

**Pro Move:** Request video of their workers packing a carton—sloppy packing causes 60% of transit damage claims. A professional packer will show you poly bags for each jean, tissue paper between folds, and proper carton sealing with reinforced tape. An amateur will stuff 50 jeans into a box with zero protection and hope for the best.

What we've seen repeatedly is that factories great at manufacturing can be terrible at logistics. They'll produce beautiful jeans and then throw them in unmarked boxes with no packing list, causing your customs broker to charge you $500 in rework fees.
