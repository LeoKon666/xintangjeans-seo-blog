## The Hidden Costs Most Guides Don't Mention

Fabric quality isn't just some technical detail. It's the difference between jeans that last two seasons and jeans your customers complain about after three washes. An 8oz fabric versus 12oz denim changes your unit cost by $1.20-$3.80 per piece, and stretch denim adds another $0.90-$1.50 compared to rigid. Nobody tells you this upfront.

Wash complexity hits harder than most people expect. A basic rinse adds $0.30 per pair—fine. Enzyme wash? That's $1.20 more. Hand-sanding jumps to an additional $2.50 per pair. These aren't optional considerations when you're trying to match the distressed look your market wants.

For shipping and duties in 2026, you're looking at sea freight running $1.20-$2.80 per pair with a 30-45 day timeline, while air express costs $4.50-$8 per pair but gets there in 7-10 days. Then add US/EU duties at 8-18% of declared value. Budget accordingly or watch your margins evaporate.

## Common Pricing Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Comparing prices without matching specs is the fastest way to make a bad decision. Always request the exact fabric weight, wash method, and hardware details. A $7 jean with 8oz fabric and plastic rivets isn't the same as a $9 jean with 10oz fabric and metal hardware—even if they look similar in photos.

Sample costs trip people up constantly. Quality factories charge $50-$120 per sample, and yes, that feels expensive until you realize it's fully refundable on bulk orders. Factories offering "free samples" are either adding the cost into your bulk pricing or cutting corners somewhere you'll notice later.

Why do payment terms matter? Because the "cheapest" suppliers often require 100% payment upfront, which means you're financing their entire operation with zero leverage if something goes wrong. We do 30% deposit, 70% before shipment. You keep control.
