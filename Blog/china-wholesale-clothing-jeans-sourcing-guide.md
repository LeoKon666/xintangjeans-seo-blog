For clothing wholesalers, this translates to 30-50% lower FOB prices than Mexican or Turkish suppliers. ## Step-by-Step: Integrating Jeans Into Clothing Orders

### Step 1: Calculate Your Denim Ratio
Most successful mixed apparel importers allocate 30-40% jeans, 30% basic tops, 20% outerwear, and 10% accessories. This balances inventory turns with shipping efficiency—pack too much of one category and you're either overstocked or understocked on complementary items.

### Step 2: Verify Supplier Capabilities
Can they produce both denim and woven tops? Do they handle combined shipments, or will you coordinate between multiple warehouses? What's the surcharge for mixed orders? Many Xintang factories now offer complete clothing packages, which simplifies the entire process if you find the right partner.

### Step 3: Optimize Shipping
Combined orders qualify for lower container utilization fees, consolidated customs clearance, and reduced per-unit freight costs. Shipping 1,000 jeans plus 700 tees costs 18% less than separate shipments—basic math that adds up quickly over a year of orders.

## Common Mistakes Clothing Buyers Make

Ignoring fabric composition is the first pitfall. Many "100% cotton" jeans contain 2-5% elastane, which is crucial for stretch but must be disclosed or you risk labeling violations. Second, size charts differ dramatically—Chinese size 30 measures 78cm waist, not the 86cm you'd expect from US size 30. Third, skipping pre-shipment inspections means 12% of first-time importers report wrong wash colors or inconsistent distressing.

To be fair, even experienced buyers sometimes miss HS code variations. Denim pants (6113.00) and denim shorts (6103.42) have different duty rates, and that 4% difference matters when you're importing containers.
