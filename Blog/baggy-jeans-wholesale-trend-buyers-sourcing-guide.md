Here's what actually matters when choosing styles.

**Fabric weight dictates quality perception.**  
Below 12oz feels cheap and loses shape quickly. The 12-14oz range works for standard commercial quality, but anything 14oz and above creates that premium drape and durability customers notice immediately.

**The pocket placement trick**  
Measure back pocket height from the crotch seam. Between 6-7 inches creates the most flattering universal placement. Drop it to 5 inches or below and you make hips look wider. Go 8 inches or above and you create a disproportionate silhouette that looks off even on the hanger.

## How to Source Baggy Jeans from China: Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Verify Factory Credentials

Request business license copies and check registration dates—not just that they exist, but when the factory actually started operating. Ask for recent quality inspection reports, not generic PDFs from 2022. Certifications like BSCI or OEKO-TEX matter more than ISO 9001 alone, which basically every factory has.

### Step 2: Order Development Samples

Typical costs run $50-120 per sample, and most factories deduct this from future orders. Production takes 7-12 days. Always request exact fabric swatches (not just color names), size spec sheets with measurements for every size you'll order, and wash instructions written specifically for the fabric blend they're using.

### Step 3: Conduct Pre-Shipment Inspection

For baggy jeans especially, check waistband tension—it should recover after stretching, not stay loose. Count stitch density at 8-12 stitches per inch for real durability. Examine wash consistency because uneven fading shows up dramatically on looser silhouettes where more fabric is visible.

### Step 4: Negotiate Logistics

Best options from Guangdong break down like this: air shipping takes 3-5 days at $5.50-$8 per kilogram; sea shipping needs 18-25 days but only costs $1.20-$1.80 per kilogram. Always insure for 110% of order value because the 10% buffer covers replacement costs if something goes wrong.

## 5 Costly Mistakes Wholesale Buyers Make (And How to Avoid Them)

Most first-time importers don't realize until their second order that certain assumptions will cost them.

**Matching fabric by description alone**  
Request physical swatches. "Medium blue stretch" varies wildly between mills, and what looks perfect on your screen arrives as completely different fabric.

**Assuming size charts are accurate**  
Have the factory measure actual production samples, not just prototypes. The cutting room sometimes uses different patterns than the sample maker, especially for larger sizes.

**Skipping pre-shipment inspections**  
What we've seen repeatedly is that 30% of inspected orders have at least one quality issue caught before shipping. That percentage drops to under 5% when buyers work with factories that expect regular inspections because they maintain better standards.

**Underestimating wash shrinkage**  
Budget 3-5% extra fabric into your calculations. Untreated denim shrinks up to 10% in the first wash, and baggy styles need that extra length to maintain the intended silhouette after customers wash them.

**Not planning for import duties**  
US denim tariffs range 16-32% depending on material composition. A $12 wholesale jean can become a $15.50 landed cost after duties, dramatically changing your margin calculations.
